NEC 2026 Structure — The Nine Chapters

Article 90 · The Road Map Every Exam Question Follows

The Most Important Rule in the NEC

Before you look up a single article, you must understand how the NEC is organized. This rule, found in NEC 90.3, governs every question on the Journeyman exam:

NEC 90.3: Chapters 1 through 4 apply generally. Chapters 5, 6, and 7 apply to special occupancies, equipment, and conditions. Where Chapters 5, 6, or 7 modify or amend Chapters 1 through 4, the Chapter 5, 6, or 7 rule applies. Chapter 8 (Communications) is independent — not subject to Chapters 1–7 except where specifically referenced. Chapter 9 (Tables) applies as referenced.

Why This Matters on the Exam

When a question involves a hazardous location (Ch. 5), a pool (Ch. 6), or an emergency system (Ch. 7), those chapters override the general rules in Chapters 1–4. If you look up the Chapter 1–4 rule only, you will get the wrong answer on those questions.

Exam Example

Q: What is the minimum conductor size for branch circuit wiring in a Class I, Division 1 hazardous location?

A student looks up Article 210 (Chapter 2) and finds #14 AWG minimum for 15A circuits.

The correct answer is in Article 501 (Chapter 5): conductors in Class I, Division 1 must be in approved wiring methods — the branch circuit rules are modified by Chapter 5.

Always check if a Chapter 5, 6, or 7 article modifies your Chapter 1–4 answer.

The NEC Hierarchy Rule — 90.3

The rule that governs every Chapter 5, 6, and 7 question

The Override Principle

NEC 90.3 establishes that Chapters 1 through 4 apply generally to all electrical installations. Chapters 5, 6, and 7 supplement or modify the general rules — and where they conflict, the Chapter 5, 6, or 7 rule takes precedence.

NEC 90.3 Code Arrangement: Chapters 1–4 → Apply to ALL electrical installations Chapters 5–7 → Special occupancies, equipment, conditions SUPPLEMENT or MODIFY Chapters 1–4 Where conflict → Chapter 5, 6, or 7 WINS Chapter 8 → Communications — independent of 1–7 Chapter 9 → Tables — apply as referenced

NEC Index Speed — Your Most Important Exam Skill

The exam is open book. Index speed separates passing students from failing ones.

The Perry 5-Step Index Method

1
Read the question twice. Underline the key noun — the most specific thing being asked about.
2
Identify your keyword. The most specific, unusual term in the question — not a generic word like "wire" or "circuit."
3
Open the index. Find your keyword alphabetically. Get the article and section number.
4
Go to the article. Read only the relevant section — not the whole article.
5
Answer and move on. Do not second-guess. Circle your answer and go to the next question.

Perry's 10-Tab System

Tab your NEC 2026 book before the next lesson. These 10 locations answer the most-tested exam topics.

Install These 10 Tabs Now

Physical tabs in your NEC book are the single most valuable exam tool you have. Each tab below is placed at the article or table that answers a high-frequency exam topic. These are not random — they are the 10 locations that come up most often on the Journeyman exam.

T1
High Leg Delta — NEC 110.15 pg. 45
The high leg (208V phase) must be identified with orange color. Always on the B phase. Questions: "What color identifies the high leg?" → Orange. "Which phase?" → B phase.
T2
Dryer and Range Demand — NEC 220.54 and 220.55 pg. 73–74
Table 220.54 = dryer demand factors. Table 220.55 = range demand factors (Column C is primary). Tab at the top of page 73 — both tables are on the same spread.
T3
Service Clearances — NEC 230.24 pg. 85
Five clearance values: 10 ft (final span residential), 12 ft (general), 15 ft (driveways), 18 ft (public streets), 3 ft (from windows). Tab here — clearance questions appear on every exam.
T4
Bonding Jumper Sizing — Table 250.102(C)(1) pg. 119
Sizes supply-side and main bonding jumpers based on largest ungrounded conductor. Different from GEC (250.66) and EGC (250.122). Tab here — the three grounding tables are a common trap.
T5
Burial Depth — Table 300.5 pg. 137
Six wiring methods × five installation conditions = 30 values. Tab here. These numbers appear 3–5 times per exam.
T6
Correction and Adjustment Factors — 310.15(B)(2) and (3) pg. 147
Temperature correction (Table 310.15(B)(2)(a)) and bundling adjustment (Table 310.15(B)(3)(a)). Both are on the same page. Tab here.
T7
Duty Cycle Table — Table 430.22(E) pg. 303
Motor conductor percentage for short-time, intermittent, periodic, and varying duty. Five columns. Tab here — duty cycle questions separate passing students from failing ones.
T8
Motor SCGFP Table — Table 430.52 pg. 308
Short-circuit and ground fault protection percentages by motor type and OCPD type. Inverse time breaker = 250% for AC single phase and polyphase. Tab here.
T9
Class II Locations — Article 502 pg. 365
Dust locations — different wiring methods than Class I. Tab at the start of Article 502. Class I (gas/vapor) and Class II (dust) questions appear on every exam.
T10
Class III Locations — Article 503 pg. 369
Easily ignitable fibers/flyings. Tab at Article 503. Together, T9 and T10 cover all three hazardous location classes.
After tabbing: Open your NEC book and flip to each tab in under 5 seconds. Do this 3 times. Your hands need to know where these pages are before exam day.
Module 1 — Lesson 1.6

Anatomy of an Exam Question

Five question types — different strategy for each

Type 1 — Direct Lookup (40% of exam)

Format: "The minimum burial depth for direct-buried cable is ___."
Strategy: Keyword → Index → Table 300.5. Time target: under 60 seconds.
The answer is in the NEC. Do not guess. Look it up every time.

Type 2 — Definition (10% of exam)

Format: "Which of the following best describes a 'listed' piece of equipment?"
Strategy: Article 100. Tab it. All definitions are there. Time target: under 30 seconds.
Key definitions to know: Listed, Labeled, Identified, Approved, Qualified Person, Service Point, Dwelling Unit.

Type 3 — Calculation (25% of exam)

Format: "What is the minimum conductor size for a 5 HP, 208V, 3-phase motor?"
Strategy: Identify the formula → look up the table value → calculate → select answer.
Write every step. Calculation errors happen when students skip steps in their head.

Type 4 — Exception (15% of exam)

Format: "Service entrance conductors shall be insulated EXCEPT for ___."
Strategy: Find the main rule → read all exceptions → match to the scenario.
The word EXCEPT is your signal. Slow down and read all options before answering.

Type 5 — Application/Scenario (10% of exam)

Format: "A garage has a floor at grade level and contains electrical equipment. What type of receptacle protection is required?"
Strategy: Identify the occupancy type and location → find the applicable rule → apply it.
These require combining multiple rules. They are the hardest questions on the exam.

Practice identifying question types on every drill in this course. When you can instantly classify a question by type, your strategy is automatic and you save time.

Go through all 80 questions. Answer anything you know in under 60 seconds without the book. Circle questions you skip. This should take 45–60 minutes and answer 30–40 questions.

Article 200 — Use and Identification of Grounded Conductors

NEC 2026 Article 200

Key Rules — Article 200

  • 200.6(A): Grounded conductors #6 AWG or smaller — identified by white or gray insulation, or white tape at termination
  • 200.6(B): Grounded conductors larger than #6 AWG — identified by white or gray tape, paint, or other markings at termination points
  • 200.7: Use of white or gray color — white/gray conductors shall not be used as ungrounded conductors except where re-identified
  • 200.9: Terminal identification — white or gray terminal screws identify the grounded conductor terminal (silver screws)

Re-identification of White/Gray Conductors

White or gray conductors used as ungrounded conductors (in switch loops, for example) must be re-identified with black tape, paint, or permanent marker at each termination and at accessible points.

Exam Example

Q: A #4 AWG white conductor is used as an ungrounded feeder conductor. How must it be identified?

Re-identified at each termination with black tape, paint, or other effective means (200.7(C)). NEC 2026 200.7(C) pg. 48

Branch Circuits — Article 210

NEC 2026 Article 210 · High-frequency exam topic

Branch Circuit Requirements — Key Rules

210.19(A) — Conductors

Branch circuit conductors shall have an ampacity not less than the maximum load to be served. For continuous loads — conductors sized at 125% of the continuous load.

210.20 — Overcurrent Protection

Branch circuit OCPD rating shall not exceed the conductor ampacity. For continuous loads — OCPD rated at 125% of continuous load (or next size up per 240.4(B)).

210.52 — Dwelling Unit Receptacle Placement

  • No point along floor line shall be more than 6 feet from a receptacle outlet
  • Wall space 2 feet wide or greater requires a receptacle
  • Countertop receptacles: every 4 feet of countertop space
  • Island/peninsular counters: at least one receptacle required if 12 in. × 24 in. or larger
6 ft and 12 ft rule: Receptacles along walls = max 6 ft from any point = max 12 ft apart. A common exam question presents both numbers — the answer depends on what is being asked (distance from a point = 6 ft; distance between receptacles = 12 ft).

210.62 — Show Window Lighting

At least one receptacle outlet for each 12 linear feet of show window.

GFCI and AFCI — All Required Locations

NEC 2026 Articles 210.8 and 210.12 · High-frequency exam topic

GFCI Protection — Article 210.8

Ground fault circuit interrupter protection is required for 15 and 20-amp, 125-volt receptacles in specific locations. The list expanded significantly in the 2017 NEC compared to prior editions.

Dwelling Units (210.8(A)) — Required GFCI Locations

  1. Bathrooms
  2. Garages and accessory buildings with a floor at or below grade
  3. Outdoors — all outdoor receptacles
  4. Crawl spaces — at or below grade level
  5. Unfinished basements
  6. Kitchens — receptacles serving countertop surfaces within 6 ft of sink
  7. Boathouses
  8. Rooftops
  9. All areas of dwelling unit where floor is within 6 ft of a pool, spa, hot tub, or fountain edge (Article 680)

Other Than Dwelling Units (210.8(B)) — Required GFCI Locations

  1. Bathrooms
  2. Kitchens — all receptacles
  3. Rooftops
  4. Outdoors — in public spaces
  5. Garages, service bays — not accessible to public
  6. Crawl spaces
  7. Unfinished portions of basements
  8. Boathouses
  9. Dishwashers
Exam trap: In dwelling units, kitchen GFCI applies to receptacles serving countertop surfaces within 6 feet of a sink (210.8(A)(6)). In commercial kitchens (210.8(B)(2)), ALL kitchen receptacles require GFCI — not just within 6 feet. Different rule for different occupancy type.

AFCI Protection — Article 210.12

Arc-fault circuit interrupter protection is required for all 120-volt, 15 and 20-amp circuits supplying outlets or devices in these locations in dwelling units:

  • Kitchens
  • Family rooms, dining rooms, living rooms, parlors, libraries, dens
  • Bedrooms
  • Sunrooms, recreation rooms, closets, hallways, laundry areas
  • Similar rooms and areas
NEC 210.12(A): All 120-volt, single-phase, 15- and 20-ampere branch circuits supplying outlets or devices installed in dwelling unit kitchens, family rooms, dining rooms, living rooms, parlors, libraries, dens, bedrooms, sunrooms, recreation rooms, closets, hallways, laundry areas, or similar rooms or areas shall be protected by a listed arc-fault circuit interrupter. Article 215 — Feeders

NEC 2026 Article 215 · Conductors between service and branch circuit panels

What Is a Feeder?

A feeder is all circuit conductors between the service equipment (or the source of a separately derived system) and the final branch circuit overcurrent protective device. Feeders supply subpanels and distribution panels.

215.2 — Minimum Ampacity

  • Feeder conductors shall have an ampacity not less than required for the load as determined by Article 220
  • For continuous loads: conductors rated at 125% of continuous load
  • Informational Note: voltage drop on feeders should not exceed 3% for best efficiency (not a requirement)

215.3 — Overcurrent Protection

Feeder conductors shall be protected by an OCPD rated or set not greater than the conductor ampacity. For continuous loads — OCPD rated at 125% of continuous load or next size up per 240.4(B).

Exam Example

Q: A feeder supplies a 200-amp continuous load. What is the minimum feeder conductor ampacity?

1
Continuous load → 125%: 200A × 1.25 = 250A minimum conductor ampacity
250A minimum conductor ampacity. Next size: 300 kcmil copper at 75°C = 285A (not sufficient) → 350 kcmil = 310A. NEC 215.2, 215.3

Article 240 — Overcurrent Protection

NEC 2026 Article 240 · Standard breaker sizes and tap rules

Table 240.6(A) — Standard Ampere Ratings

OCPD must be selected from the standard ampere ratings in Table 240.6(A). If the calculated ampacity does not correspond to a standard rating, select the next size up (240.4(B) — within limits).

Standard ratings (Table 240.6(A)): 15, 20, 25, 30, 35, 40, 45, 50, 60, 70, 80, 90, 100, 110, 125, 150, 175, 200, 225, 250, 300, 350, 400, 450, 500, 600, 700, 800, 1000, 1200, 1600, 2000, 2500, 3000, 4000, 5000, 6000 amperes

240.4(B) — Next Size Up Rule

Overcurrent protection for conductors not rated 800A or less may be the next higher standard rating if the conductor ampacity does not correspond to a standard rating — and if the conductor is not part of a multi-outlet branch circuit supplying receptacles for cord-and-plug-connected loads.

240.21(B) — Tap Conductor Rules

  • 10-foot tap: Tap conductors not over 10 ft long, ampacity ≥ combined loads, terminates in single OCPD, enclosed in raceway
  • 25-foot tap: Tap conductors not over 25 ft, ampacity ≥ 1/3 of feeder OCPD rating, terminates in single OCPD, in raceway

Service Overcurrent Protection

230.90 — Where Required

Each ungrounded service conductor shall have overcurrent protection. The OCPD shall be connected in series with each ungrounded service conductor. Rating shall not exceed the conductor ampacity.

GFPE shall be provided for solidly grounded wye services of more than 150 volts to ground but not exceeding 600 volts phase-to-phase for each service disconnect rated 1,000 amps or more.

GFPE is required when ALL of these apply:
  • Solidly grounded wye service (e.g., 480Y/277V)
  • Voltage exceeds 150V to ground
  • Service disconnect rated 1,000A or more
GFPE is NOT required for: 120/240V single-phase services, 120/208V services, or services with disconnects rated under 1,000A.
The GFPE trap: GFPE (230.95) protects equipment, not people. It trips at very low fault currents (typically 1200–5000 mA) — much lower than breaker ratings — to prevent equipment damage from arcing ground faults. Do not confuse with GFCI (210.8) which protects people at 6 mA trip level.

Ground Fault Protection of Equipment — 230.95

1,000A+ services on 480Y/277V systems

The Complete GFPE Rule

NEC 230.95: Ground-Fault Protection of Equipment Required for solidly grounded wye electrical services of: - More than 150 volts to ground (480Y/277V qualifies; 120/208Y does not) - Not exceeding 600 volts phase-to-phase - Service disconnect rated 1,000 amps or more Exceptions: - Continuous industrial processes where shutdown would create greater hazard - Fire pumps

Why 480Y/277V and Not 120/208Y?

The 480Y/277V system has 277 volts to ground — exceeding the 150V threshold. The 120/208Y system has 120 volts to ground — below the 150V threshold and not subject to 230.95.

Testing Requirement

When GFPE is first installed, it shall be performance tested. The test shall be conducted by a qualified person using a current injection method specified by the manufacturer.

System Grounding — NEC 250 Part II

When systems must be grounded and why

250.20 — AC Systems Required to Be Grounded

The following alternating current systems must be grounded:

  • 120/240V single-phase systems
  • 120/208V three-phase wye systems
  • 120/240V three-phase delta systems with a grounded conductor (high-leg)
  • 277/480V three-phase wye systems

250.24 — Grounding Service-Supplied AC Systems

The grounded conductor (neutral) shall be connected to the grounding electrode system at the service equipment. The grounded conductor is bonded to the equipment grounding conductor and the grounding electrode conductor at the main bonding jumper location — at the service equipment only.

Neutral-ground bond: The neutral is bonded to the equipment ground ONLY at the service equipment (main panel). In subpanels, the neutral must be isolated from the equipment ground. This is one of the most common field errors and exam questions.

250.28 — Main Bonding Jumper

The main bonding jumper connects the equipment grounding conductor (or grounding bus) to the grounded service conductor (neutral) at the service. It must be a wire, bus, screw, or similar conductor. If a screw, it must be green in color.

Motor Circuit Components

NEC 2026 Article 430 · The four required components

The Four Components of a Motor Branch Circuit

Every motor branch circuit requires four separate protective/control elements, in this order from the panel to the motor:

1
Branch Circuit Conductors — sized at 125% of motor FLC (430.22)
2
Short-Circuit and Ground-Fault Protection (SCGFP) — sized per Table 430.52 (fuses or breaker). Sized LARGER than overload. Permits next size up.

Sealing Fittings — NEC 501.15

Class I Hazardous Locations · Required locations and installation

Why Sealing Fittings Are Required

In Class I locations, flammable gases can travel through conduit and enter non-classified areas or equipment enclosures. Sealing fittings prevent this gas migration and contain any explosion that might occur within explosionproof equipment.

501.15(A) — Where Required in Division 1

  • Within 18 inches of every explosionproof enclosure where conduit enters
  • In each conduit run leaving a Class I Division 1 area
  • Each conduit run 2-inch trade size or larger entering an enclosure with terminals, splices, or taps

501.15(B) — Where Required in Division 2

  • Where conduit passes from a Division 2 into an unclassified area — seals required unless a fitting between the conduit and the unclassified location is identified for the purpose

Sealing Compound Requirements

Sealing compound must be resistant to the specific gas or vapor and must not be affected by the liquids present. Sealing compound must have a minimum melting point of 93°C (200°F) and must fill the conduit cross-section for a minimum depth equal to the trade size of the conduit, or 1 inch — whichever is greater.

18-inch rule: Sealing fittings in Class I locations must be within 18 inches of every explosionproof enclosure. This specific measurement appears on exams. No splices or taps are permitted in the sealing fitting.

Key Definitions (Article 690)

  • PV system: Total components and subsystems that convert solar energy into electrical energy

PV Conductor Sizing — 690.8

Two calculation steps:
  1. Maximum current = Isc × 1.25 (125% of short circuit current)
  2. Conductor ampacity = Maximum current × 1.25 (125% continuous load factor)
  3. Combined: conductor sized at 156% of Isc (1.25 × 1.25 = 1.5625)

The maximum PV system voltage for dwellings is 600 volts. For other than dwellings, the maximum is 1,000 volts (or higher if listed equipment is used).

NEC 690.7(A): In a dc PV source circuit or PV output circuit, the maximum voltage shall be considered as the highest voltage between any two conductors. For crystalline and multicrystalline silicon modules, this is determined using the temperature coefficient for Voc.

Ohm's Law and Power — The Foundation

E = I × R · P = I × E · The formulas every calculation starts from

Ohm's Law — Three Forms

E = I × R (Volts = Amps × Ohms) I = E ÷ R (Amps = Volts ÷ Ohms) R = E ÷ I (Ohms = Volts ÷ Amps)

Power Formula — Four Forms

P = I × E (Watts = Amps × Volts) P = I² × R (Watts = Amps² × Ohms) P = E² ÷ R (Watts = Volts² ÷ Ohms) I = P ÷ E (Amps = Watts ÷ Volts)

VA vs Watts — The NEC Uses VA

For DC circuits: VA = Watts (power factor = 1, 100%).
For AC circuits: Watts = VA × power factor. The NEC uses VA for load calculations because it works for both resistive and reactive loads without needing to know the power factor.

Key fact: In NEC load calculations, 1 VA = 1 Watt for calculation purposes. The NEC 2026 uses VA throughout Article 220 load calculations.
Exam Example

Q: What is the resistance of a 120V circuit with a 15-amp load?

1
R = E ÷ I
2
R = 120V ÷ 15A = 8 ohms
8 ohms. R = E ÷ I per Ohm's Law.

Module 13 — Lesson 13.1

Top 10 Journeyman Exam Traps

The mistakes that cause the most failures — and how to avoid every one

The 10 Most Common Journeyman Exam Errors

Trap 1 — Using Nameplate FLA for Motor Conductors

The exam gives you a nameplate FLA and asks for conductor size. Students use the nameplate. Never. Use Table 430.248 or 430.250 FLC. Nameplate FLA is only for overload protection (430.32).

Trap 2 — Using the Wrong Grounding Table

GEC → Table 250.66 (input: conductor size). EGC → Table 250.122 (input: OCPD rating). Bonding jumper → Table 250.102(C)(1) (input: ungrounded conductor size). Three conductors. Three tables. Three different inputs.

Trap 3 — Confusing Division 1 and Division 2

Division 1 = hazardous atmosphere NORMALLY present. Division 2 = only under ABNORMAL conditions. Division 1 is MORE restrictive. Exam questions frequently reverse these.

Trap 4 — Applying 70% as a Range or Dryer Demand Factor

70% is the neutral conductor reduction factor (220.61(B)(1)), not a demand factor. Range demand = Table 220.55 Column C (a fixed kW value). Dryer demand = Table 220.54 (a percentage based on quantity).

Trap 5 — Single-Phase Formula on a Three-Phase System

For voltage drop: single-phase = 2 × K × I × D / CM. Three-phase = 1.732 × K × I × D / CM. For service amps: single-phase = VA ÷ V. Three-phase = VA ÷ (V × 1.732). Always identify the system first.

Trap 6 — The Informational Note Trap

Informational notes in the NEC are NOT requirements. Questions like "what does the NEC require for voltage drop?" — the NEC does not require a specific percentage. 210.19(A) has an informational note recommending 3% but it is not mandatory.

Trap 7 — Forgetting the 125% Rule for Continuous Loads

Any load expected to operate continuously for 3 hours or more = continuous load. Conductors and OCPD must be sized at 125% of the continuous load (210.20(A), 215.3).

Trap 8 — The 75% Appliance Rule Applies Only to Dwelling Units

220.53: 75% demand factor for 4 or more fastened-in-place appliances applies to dwelling units only. Commercial kitchens, offices, and other occupancies do not get this demand factor.

Trap 9 — Chapter 5 Overrides Chapter 1–4

When a question involves a hazardous location, healthcare facility, or mobile home park — always check the Chapter 5 article first. It may require more restrictive conditions than the general rule.

Trap 10 — Confusing the Optional Method Scope

NEC 220.82 (Optional Method) applies ONLY to dwelling units with 100A+ single-phase service. Commercial occupancies must use the Standard Method. No Optional Method for churches, offices, or schools.

The elimination strategy: On any multiple-choice question, eliminate the two obviously wrong answers first. Between the remaining two, the one with an NEC citation you can verify in under 60 seconds is almost always correct. Never leave a question blank — if truly unsure, guess from your two finalists.

Module 13 — Lesson 13.3

NEC Key Word Traps

Shall · Permitted · Need Not · Listed · Identified · Approved

The Six Most Important NEC Words

SHALL

Mandatory requirement. "Conductors shall be protected." There is no choice. This is a code violation if not done.

SHALL NOT

Absolute prohibition. "Vegetation shall not be used for support." No exception unless explicitly stated.

PERMITTED

Allowed but not required. "This wiring method shall be permitted in..." means you MAY use it, not that you must.

NEED NOT

An exception to a requirement. "The disconnect need not be within sight if it is lockable." Means a rule that normally applies does not apply in this situation.

LISTED

Equipment examined and found to meet specific requirements by a nationally recognized testing laboratory (NRTL). The label is on the equipment. Examples: UL Listed, CSA Listed.

APPROVED

Acceptable to the Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ). Listed equipment is usually approved, but the AHJ has final authority. The AHJ can approve unlisted equipment and can reject listed equipment.

Listed vs Approved trap: Listed is objective (NRTL testing). Approved is subjective (AHJ decision). An AHJ can approve unlisted equipment, and can disapprove listed equipment. Questions that confuse these two terms are common on exams.

Exam Application

When you see "shall" in an answer choice about something that is actually only "permitted" in the NEC — that answer is wrong. The word choice in NEC questions is precise and intentional.

Exception Traps and Informational Note Traps

The two most missed question types on the Journeyman exam

The Exception Trap

Every NEC rule is followed by exceptions that modify or override it. Exam questions are specifically designed to test whether you know the exception, not just the main rule.

How to spot exception trap questions: The question will give you a scenario that sounds like it should apply the main rule, but one detail makes an exception applicable. Look for words like "except," "unless," "where," "provided," and "other than."
Exception Trap Example

Q: Service-entrance conductors shall be insulated. This requirement does NOT apply to:

A. Conductors entering a weatherhead   B. The grounded conductor in a cable assembly   C. Conductors in conduit   D. Underground laterals

B — The grounded service conductor in a cable assembly is permitted to be bare (230.41 Exception). The exception modifies the main rule. NEC 230.41 pg. 87

The Informational Note Trap

Informational notes in the NEC are explanatory — they are NOT requirements. They appear throughout the code and frequently become the wrong answer on exam questions.
